Columbia expands course schedule ‘de-densification,’ capping proportion of classes in prime midday slots

The University expanded the program to all undergraduate departments after previously piloting with 19 departments in spring 2026, following decades of frustrations over limited classroom space.

By Matt Luo and Celine Chien

Graphic by Arabelle Park and Emily Ta

April 23, 2026

Zohar Ford / Deputy Photo Editor

Columbia has expanded its program to “de-densify” the class schedule to all undergraduate departments for fall 2026. The University is now enforcing a 40 percent cap on the proportion of courses departments can offer in the prime midday window between 10 a.m. and 2 p.m.

The University first piloted the program with 19 of its largest departments within the Faculty of Arts and Sciences and the School of Engineering and Applied Science for spring 2026. The measure followed decades of mounting frustrations among faculty members and students with course scheduling and limited classroom inventory.

Columbia announced the program’s expansion in a March 11 email to faculty members, citing it as part of the University’s efforts to accommodate its planned expansion of the undergraduate student body in Columbia College and SEAS. The plan, which would add 125, 200, and then 250 students to the next three first-year classes, has drawn fierce backlash from students, faculty members, and alumni over the past several months over criticism that it would push the University’s physical space to its limits, among other concerns.

University leaders wrote in the March 11 email that the spring 2026 pilot program offered “more choices to students” while proceeding “without any negative instructional impact.” A December 2025 Spectator analysis found that most departments that participated in the pilot only had to shift a minimal number of classes to comply with the cap.

“We could talk in very general terms about de-densification, scheduling, but at the end of the day, this becomes a very personal problem for students and faculty the minute you want to take courses that you can’t because they’re conflicting with each other, or the minute a course has to be capped because it can’t fit into a larger room,” Barry Kane, associate vice president and University registrar, said in an interview with Spectator. “Those are problems that students feel every day.”

Faculty committees have attempted to address course scheduling issues since at least 1998. However, several of these efforts failed due to a lack of enforcement and oversight. Most recently, the course access and scheduling committee, which convened in October 2023 and issued its final report in February 2025, chaired by political science professor Gregory Wawro, recommended de-densification as a way to address the “collective action problem” of course scheduling at the University.

“We realize that any time you change practices, things people are used to doing, it’s going to be hard for people to accept,” Wawro said. “So you need to convince them that there needs to be change for the greater good of the community.”

Kane said that the registrar’s office has expanded efforts to inform the Columbia community about the problem.

“When folks are aware of the problem and what is causing it, they’re much more likely to be part of the solution, and that’s how we ended up being able to launch this de-densification project, because we had the agreement and the cooperation of the departments,” Kane said. “We’re all aware of the problem, we’re all willing to act.”

A University official told Spectator that de-densification currently only applies to departments, not institutes or centers, and that it was meant as a first step toward a more comprehensive and nuanced model, which would require a broader effort. The program also does not include discussion sections, teaching labs, or independent study.

A Spectator analysis, based on data scraped from the class directory on Wednesday, after course registration had concluded, found that the overall percentage of courses that occurred during the peak window decreased from approximately 45.8 percent to around 43.3 percent between fall 2025 and fall 2026.

A University official told Spectator that the course schedule remains in flux and the curriculum would still be changed as Columbia adds more classes and as faculty members start or become unavailable.

When assessing individual departments’ course offerings, Spectator found that 30 departments, out of the 37 departments in the FAS and SEAS, were above the 40 percent cap for fall 2026, compared to 29 in fall 2025, and 25 in spring 2026.

The biomedical engineering department held the highest percentage—73.91 percent—of its classes within the prime midday class window in fall 2026, followed by the applied physics and applied mathematics department.

The sociology; earth and environmental engineering; and ecology, evolution, and environmental biology departments all ranked among the departments with the lowest proportion of classes—between 21.42 and 35.29 percent—starting in the 10 a.m. to 2 p.m de-densification period. The Center for the Core Curriculum, which is not a department, had 34.94 percent of its classes within this window.

University leaders also mentioned that Columbia was “adding more sections to ease scheduling conflicts” in their March 11 email to faculty announcing expansion.

The registrar’s office previously indicated to faculty members that it believed that Columbia’s classroom capacity was sufficient to accommodate expansion. Kane said that after modeling both a 13 percent and 20 percent increase in undergraduate enrollment, he “didn’t see a scenario developing that was substantially more difficult than what we normally deal with.” The University had originally considered expanding undergraduate enrollment by up to 20 percent starting in summer 2025, but it scaled back these plans in late February.

The University has added just under 40 classrooms on the Morningside campus since 2011, with two spaces in Chandler Hall, nine in Lerner Hall, six in the Kraft Center, 16 spaces in Uris Hall, and, most recently, nine new classrooms in the Martin Luther King building at Riverside Church, according to Kane.

Kane said that he has always been able to find some sort of solution to find a classroom for “unplaced courses” or classes which the registrar’s optimization model is not able to match to a classroom automatically, requiring manual intervention. He added that the University has never canceled a class because they could not find a classroom for it.

“The number of unplaced courses and the type of unplaced courses did not move it into a category where I was prepared to say, ‘Oh, well, this will never work,’ or ‘We would never be able to proceed with those increases,’” Kane said, in reference to the optimizations he ran to model expansion.

Typically, the registrar must negotiate to find classrooms for these unplaced courses, make use of “sequestered” space—rooms that fall under departments’ control, not the registrar—or ask departments to cap course registrations or change meeting times.

The FAS has historically faced financial strain as the University increased demands on its budget to fund the construction of the Manhattanville campus, despite the unit receiving little benefit from Manhattanville itself. The cost of renting classroom space from other University schools and spaces across the neighborhood has only exacerbated this strain.

Wawro said the financial burden of renting classroom space for the FAS “figured prominently” in the course access committee’s work, which found that the University relied on rental spaces even for time slots outside the prime window de-densification targeted.

The FAS began using Lerner classrooms in 2019, and established a flat-rate contract for the nine classrooms in fall 2022. Kane noted how the registrar “worked carefully” to negotiate with University Event Management to leave room for student events, which usually occur in the evening.

“I really liked the idea of having Lerner as a really all-purpose kind of space where students would not only eat in Lerner and have events in Lerner, but would go to class in Lerner,” Kane said.

Faculty members and students had previously expressed concerns about the impact de-densification would have both on faculty members with family responsibilities and students’ mental health, due to the extended class day.

Wawro said that faculty members’ family responsibilities were “a major topic of discussion” for the committee, which ultimately recommended granting exceptions on a case by case basis.

“We knew that this requires some sacrifice,” Wawro said. “Shared sacrifice is worth it, and we can do this self-reflection, do this kind of work, and change can actually happen at a place like Columbia.”
